Has Nepal been a gerontocracy?

With the recent dissolution of parliament quashed by the supreme court and the incessant political unrest in the nation, I was trying to rationalize how we ended up in this predicament.To unravel my curiosity, I headed over to the official website of the prime minister of Nepal to take a look at the council of ministers. However, the website didn’t have sufficient information, but I was motivated to dig deep and learn more about the ministers and politicians of Nepal. So, after browsing through a good number of government websites, I planned to find the age of the politicians.

I failed to find any reliable source that hosted data for politicians of Nepal. Luckily, I found a Wikipedia page that listed the politicians of Nepal. So, scraping the data from Wikipedia was my best bet. The link to the repository that scrapes the age is here.

Caveat: The final list only accounts for the politicians that are alive. Also, the list fails to capture all of the politicians. I was able to retrieve information of a mere 54 politician.

Surprisingly, a few important things can be inferred from this small sample.

The youngest politician is Ranju Darshana, aged 24, while the oldest politician is Khadgajeet Baral, aged 92. Thus, the mean age of politicians is 63 (rounded from 62.87). It is interesting to note that the Senior Citizen Act of Nepal 2006 defines “Senior Citizen” as a citizen of Nepal having completed the age of sixty years. This certainly proves that Nepal has been a gerontocracy.

The Global Parliamentary Report by UNDP highlights the global average age of a Member of Parliament (MP) as 53. The average age for a woman MP is 50. Sub-Saharan African MPs have the lowest regional average age at 49, with Arab countries with the highest at 55. The average age of Nepali politicians (i.e., 63) is higher than any other nation.

The sample data validates that Nepali youth have many barriers to pursuing politics. The old generation must step up and be mindful to teach their subordinates.
